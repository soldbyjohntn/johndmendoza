The Local Playbook

The Smoky Mountain STR Investor Guide

Permits, zoning, fire code, financing, and the tax strategy — everything I walk my investor clients through before they buy a cabin in Sevier County, written down in one place.

Why Here

12+ million visitors a year don't need convincing

The Great Smoky Mountains is the most-visited national park in the country, and Sevier County — Sevierville, Pigeon Forge, and Gatlinburg — is its front door. That visitor volume is why this market supports one of the densest short-term rental economies in the U.S., and why cabins here trade as income property first and vacation homes second.

But the market punishes buyers who treat every cabin the same. Where the cabin sits determines what permits it needs, what it can legally sleep, and what it will actually earn. That's what this guide covers.

Permits & Zoning

Every jurisdiction plays by different rules

"Sevier County" is really four regulatory environments. The same cabin moved a mile down the road can face a completely different permit process — or be ineligible to rent at all.

Unincorporated Sevier County

Short-term rentals operate under the county's STRU permit program. Permitting, inspections, and renewal requirements apply — and permit status should be verified on any resale cabin before you write an offer.

Pigeon Forge

City zoning controls where overnight rentals are allowed. Many cabin communities are zoned for it; pockets are not. Never assume — verify the parcel's zoning with the city before going under contract.

Gatlinburg

Long-established tourist rental market with its own city permitting and inspection regime. Density and parking can be the gotchas here, especially in older chalets.

Sevierville

City limits have their own rules separate from the county. Newer resort communities (Shagbark-style gated developments) are often the cleanest path — built and platted with STR use in mind.

The 12-guest threshold

Tennessee fire code treats large-occupancy rentals differently: once a cabin sleeps roughly 13 or more guests, it can cross into commercial fire code territory — sprinkler systems, commercial egress, and a very different inspection standard. Big lodges earn the most gross revenue in this market, but the occupancy number on the listing and the occupancy number the fire marshal will sign off on are not always the same thing. I check this on every large-group property before my clients offer.

The Numbers

How I underwrite a cabin

A listing agent's revenue claim is a starting point, not a number you borrow against. Before any client of mine writes an offer, we build the real picture:

  • Revenue comps, not projections. Actual performance data from comparable cabins — same bedroom count, same amenity tier, same micro-area — using AirDNA and real host data, not the broker's brochure.
  • Amenities priced honestly. Indoor pools, theater rooms, game lofts, and hot tubs each move nightly rate and occupancy differently. An indoor pool is the single strongest converter in this market.
  • Full expense load. Management (or your time), cleaning, utilities, cable/internet, hot tub service, pest control, capex reserve, insurance at STR rates — not homeowner rates.
  • Financing modeled both ways. Conventional second-home versus DSCR investor loans, at real current rates, so you know your debt service coverage before you commit.
  • Exit and stress tests. What happens to your numbers at 10% lower occupancy? If rates move? If the county adds a fee? If it only works in the best case, it doesn't work.
The Tax Angle

Why high earners buy cabins here

Short-term rentals occupy a unique spot in the tax code. Because average guest stays run under seven days, an STR can avoid the passive-loss limitations that apply to traditional rentals — meaning that with material participation, depreciation losses may offset active income like W-2 wages or business profits.

Pair that with a cost segregation study and bonus depreciation, and a significant share of a cabin's purchase price can potentially be written off far faster than the standard 27.5-year schedule. For buyers in high tax brackets, this strategy is often the difference-maker on whether a deal pencils.
